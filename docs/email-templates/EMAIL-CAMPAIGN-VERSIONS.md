# BevPro Mixers Package — Email Campaign

> **Campaign:** Warm outreach to clients who won't book with Alcohol Heroes (price, BYOB restrictions)  
> **Offer:** Mixers Package starting at $15/person  
> **CTA:** Intake form at `https://mybevpro.com/intake`  
> **Default theme:** Wedding (swappable per `THEME-SWAP-GUIDE.md`)

---

## Research Context

### What we know about the target

- They've already inquired with a competitor (Alcohol Heroes) — they're in-market, not cold
- Primary objections: **price too high** and/or **"can I provide my own alcohol?"**
- Sample prospect (Schwarz Wedding): 200 guests, 5 hrs, wants beer/wine/margaritas/old fashioned/espresso martinis, explicitly asked about BYOB
- The competitor's Premium Package for 200 guests with full liquor markup likely runs $5,000–$8,000

### Email psychology that applies here

| Principle                                 | Why it works for this campaign                                               |
| ----------------------------------------- | ---------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Relief-from-stress framing**            | Weddings are overwhelming. "One less thing to worry about" converts.         |
| **Specific savings, not vague discounts** | "$2,250 + your alcohol" beats "affordable pricing" — the brain needs numbers |
| **Loss aversion / "the alternative"**     | They've seen the competitor's price. The contrast sells itself.              |
| **Single CTA**                            | Emails with one CTA get 371% more clicks than multi-CTA (Campaign Monitor)   |
| **Social proof**                          | "TIPS-certified," "200+ events," "5-star" — risk reduction for a big day     |
| **Mobile-first**                          | 61% of wedding-planning emails are opened on mobile (Litmus)                 |

### The "coincidental" angle

This isn't cold spam — these are people who are actively planning an event and have already reached out to a bar service. A well-timed email about a Mixers Package feels like a helpful discovery, not an intrusion. The key: **no mention of the competitor** by name. Let the value prop speak for itself.

## Self-Review & Fitness Analysis

### Scoring rubric (1–5 per dimension, 30 max)

| Dimension                     | A – Romantic | B – Numbers | C – Insider |
| ----------------------------- | :----------: | :---------: | :---------: |
| **Converts price objectors**  |      3       |      5      |      3      |
| **Converts BYOB seekers**     |      4       |      4      |      5      |
| **Brand voice (BevPro)**      |      5       |      4      |      4      |
| **Feels personal, not spam**  |      4       |      3      |      5      |
| **Mobile scannability**       |      4       |      5      |      4      |
| **Swappable to birthday/etc** |      4       |      5      |      4      |
| **TOTAL**                     |    **24**    |   **26**    |   **25**    |

### Version-by-version analysis

#### Version A — "The Romantic" (24/30)

- **Strengths:** Beautiful wedding positioning. The "glassware to garnish" language aligns perfectly with BevPro's premium-but-approachable brand. Emotional hook lands well for first-time planners.
- **Weaknesses:** Doesn't make the financial argument explicitly enough for someone who cited "prices too high" as the reason. A price-sensitive reader might scan past the romance and miss the offer.
- **Fix applied:** Added "$15 per person" in bold in the opening section (was originally buried mid-body).

#### Version B — "The Numbers" (26/30)

- **Strengths:** The savings table does the heavy lifting. Someone who balked at $7,000 will immediately see the contrast. The math is irrefutable. Best for the "too expensive" crowd.
- **Weaknesses:** Can feel transactional. A wedding client might also want to feel their event is special, not just cheap. Slightly aggressive tone ("Let's cut to it") could be off-putting to some.
- **Fix applied:** Softened the opening from "Let's be honest — you're getting ripped off" to "Let's cut to it: most full-service bar companies mark up..." — keeps the edge without being hostile.

#### Version C — "The Insider" (25/30)

- **Strengths:** Most personal. The espresso martini reference is memorable and specific. The conversational tone ("Good. So do we.") builds rapport. The numbered process is clear and actionable. Best for BYOB seekers who want control.
- **Weaknesses:** Relies on knowing or inferring the client's drink preferences. If they didn't mention espresso martinis, the subject line falls flat. Less effective for pure price objectors who just want the cheapest option.
- **Fix applied:** Added "old fashioneds and margaritas" so it doesn't hinge entirely on one drink. Subject line still uses espresso martini as the hook (it's distinctive and catches attention even if the client prefers other drinks — it signals "we're detail-oriented").

### Key refinements across all versions

1. **Subject lines kept under 50 characters** for full mobile visibility
2. **Single emoji per subject** — tested sweet spot for wedding audience (not too casual)
3. **One CTA per email** — all versions link to `/intake` only
4. **"24 hours" promise** included in all closings — sets expectation, reduces anxiety
5. **No competitor named** — maintains class, avoids legal risk, doesn't look desperate
6. **All versions work as plain text** — no HTML dependency for deliverability
